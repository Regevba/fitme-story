# 13 UX Principles That Govern Every Screen

## Context

This document defines the UX foundations for a fitness tracking app -- not what it looks like, but how it behaves. Visual tokens describe appearance; these 13 principles describe experience. They are consulted before writing any new screen, before validating any feature, and before resolving any UX disagreement.

The principles fall into two groups: 8 universal UX heuristics applied to the fitness domain, and 5 domain-specific principles that encode what makes fitness apps succeed or fail.

---

## Part 1: Design Philosophy and Principles

### Core Principles (8) -- Universal UX Heuristics Applied to Fitness

#### 1.1 Fitts's Law

**Definition:** The time to acquire a target is a function of the distance to and size of the target. Larger and closer targets are faster to reach.

**Why it matters for fitness:** Users log data with sweaty hands, sometimes wearing gloves, often mid-workout. Touch precision is degraded. Primary actions must be impossible to miss.

**Application:**
- Set logging buttons are full-row tappable (not just a small checkmark icon)
- Primary CTAs (Get Started, Continue, Save) anchor to the bottom of the screen for thumb reach, 52pt height
- Tab bar uses standard 49pt height with 44pt minimum tap target per tab

**Do:** Make primary actions full-width and bottom-anchored. Size touch targets for imprecise input.

**Don't:** Place small buttons in hard-to-reach positions. Don't assume precise taps during a workout.

---

#### 1.2 Hick's Law

**Definition:** The time it takes to make a decision increases with the number and complexity of choices.

**Why it matters for fitness:** Users opening the app mid-rest-break have 30-60 seconds before their next set. Decision paralysis kills momentum.

**Application:**
- Onboarding goal selection: exactly 4 options -- not 10
- Home screen surfaces ONE primary action: today's workout or today's meal log
- Settings groups 19 options into 9 categories -- never a flat list

**Do:** Limit to max 4-6 options per screen. Group related items. Use progressive disclosure for advanced features.

**Don't:** Show every available action on the home screen. Force users to scan a long list to find the common case.

---

#### 1.3 Jakob's Law

**Definition:** Users spend most of their time on other apps. They expect your app to work the same way.

**Why it matters for fitness:** A fitness app competes with Strava, Apple Health, MyFitnessPal, Hevy. Users have muscle memory for tab bars at the bottom, swipe-back navigation, sheets with grab handles, search at the top of lists.

**Application:**
- Bottom tab bar (platform standard) -- not a custom hamburger menu
- Push navigation with swipe-back gesture
- Sheets dismiss with swipe-down (matches modal convention)
- Settings looks like the platform Settings app (grouped categories with chevrons)

**Do:** Follow platform Human Interface Guidelines. Use native components when possible.

**Don't:** Invent custom navigation patterns. Don't put the back button in the bottom-right because it "feels modern."

---

#### 1.4 Progressive Disclosure

**Definition:** Show only what's necessary in the moment. Reveal complexity on demand.

**Why it matters for fitness:** Health data is dense. A complete biometric panel has 10+ values (HR, HRV, sleep duration, sleep quality, RHR, weight, body fat, hydration, mood, soreness). Showing all of them upfront overwhelms users.

**Application:**
- A readiness card shows ONE number (readiness score) by default. Tap to reveal the full breakdown (HRV, sleep, RHR, recovery trend, training load, stress).
- Metric cards show headline value plus trend arrow. Tap to drill into a chart with full history.
- Onboarding asks ONE question per screen, not 10 fields on a single form.

**Real-world example:** Apple Health summary cards show one metric plus sparkline plus trend arrow. Tapping reveals a full chart. Tapping the chart reveals individual data points.

**Do:** Lead with the headline. Make detail one tap away.

**Don't:** Cram every available field into the first view. Don't make users learn the app to use it.

---

#### 1.5 Recognition Over Recall

**Definition:** Visible options are easier than memorized commands. Show users what they can do; don't make them remember.

**Why it matters for fitness:** Users open the app once a day for roughly 3 minutes during a workout. They forget where features live. Hidden gestures and memorized commands fail.

**Application:**
- Day-type badge always visible on home (Push / Pull / Legs / Rest) -- users don't need to remember today's split
- Macro progress bars show current vs. target -- users don't need to remember their daily protein goal
- Streak counter on home -- users see their consistency without checking history

**Do:** Make state visible. Use badges, indicators, progress bars.

**Don't:** Hide critical state behind tabs or modals. Don't require users to "go check" their goal.

---

#### 1.6 Consistency

**Definition:** Internal consistency (the app's own patterns) and external consistency (platform conventions).

**Why it matters for fitness:** Users build muscle memory. If the "save" button is bottom-right on one screen and top-right on another, every tap becomes a hesitation.

**Application:**
- Card layout consistent: shared card component with fixed radius, shadow, and internal padding
- Section headers consistent: shared component, never raw text with custom styling
- Button styles consistent: shared button with defined hierarchies (primary, secondary, tertiary, destructive)

**Do:** Use shared components. Reference component contracts before creating new patterns.

**Don't:** Style each screen differently because "this one is special."

---

#### 1.7 Feedback

**Definition:** Every user action gets an immediate response (visual, haptic, or audio) within 100ms.

**Why it matters for fitness:** During a workout, users don't have time to wonder "did that save?" They need instant confirmation.

**Application:**
- Set completion: medium haptic + checkmark animation + row slides to "completed" state
- PR achieved: success haptic + brief glow animation on the metric
- Save action: bottom toast confirmation ("Workout saved")
- Sync in progress: branded logo loader with breathing animation
- Button press: light haptic, brief opacity fade

**Do:** Pair every action with feedback. Use motion tokens and haptic generators.

**Don't:** Leave users wondering. Don't queue feedback for after a network round-trip.

---

#### 1.8 Error Prevention

**Definition:** Design to prevent mistakes, not just handle them after the fact.

**Why it matters for fitness:** Health data is precious. A user who accidentally deletes 3 months of workout history will never trust the app again.

**Application:**
- Account deletion has a 30-day grace period -- users can recover within the window
- Destructive actions require a confirmation modal with an explicit "I understand" toggle
- Form validation happens inline as the user types, not on submit
- Sync conflicts surface a diff view, not a "your data was overwritten" message

**Do:** Make destructive actions reversible. Confirm before deleting. Validate as you type.

**Don't:** Show a generic error after the user tapped "Delete." Don't validate only on submit.

### Domain-Specific Principles (5) -- What Makes Fitness Apps Succeed or Fail

#### 1.9 Readiness-First

**Definition:** Always lead with "how am I doing today?" before "what should I do?"

**Why this principle exists:** Recovery and readiness are the competitive moat. Strava shows distance. MyFitnessPal shows calories. Hevy shows volume. Leading with how the user feels, then guiding what they should do based on that signal, is the differentiator.

**Application:**
- Home screen layout: readiness card is the first card, before today's workout
- Training plan adapts to readiness: low readiness suggests deload; high readiness suggests PR attempt
- Stats default view: recovery metrics (HRV, sleep) above performance metrics (volume, PRs)

**Do:** Open with the user's state. Let the data inform what comes next.

**Don't:** Force users into "today's workout" without acknowledging fatigue. Don't bury readiness behind a tab.

---

#### 1.10 Zero-Friction Logging

**Definition:** Every data entry should be completable in under 10 seconds.

**Why this principle exists:** Fitness apps fail when logging becomes a chore. The best workout log is the one that's actually used. The winning apps share one trait: minimal friction from intent to logged data.

**Application:**
- Set logging: previous values auto-populate (weight, reps from last session)
- Meal entry: 4 entry methods (smart capture, manual, template, search) -- pick the fastest for the situation
- Biometric entry: health platform auto-syncs HR/HRV/sleep -- manual entry only for things it doesn't capture
- Quick actions: home screen has one-tap access to "Log meal" and "Start workout"

**Do:** Pre-fill everything you can. Default to the user's most common case. Make the happy path one tap.

**Don't:** Make users re-enter values they entered last week. Don't show empty forms.

---

#### 1.11 Privacy by Default

**Definition:** Encrypt first, explain later. Health data never leaves the device unencrypted. Analytics never see raw values.

**Why this principle exists:** Health data is the most sensitive personal data category. Users trust the app with workout history, weight, body fat, sleep patterns, mood. A single leak destroys that trust.

**Application:**
- All sync uses strong encryption (AES-256-GCM + ChaCha20-Poly1305)
- Analytics events never include raw health values -- only categorical bands ("logged_workout", not "logged 200lb bench press")
- Tracking consent is explicit, granular, and reversible
- "Privacy" and "Encryption" are visible labels in Settings, not buried in legal text

**Do:** Encrypt by default. Surface privacy as a feature, not a disclosure.

**Don't:** Send raw health data to analytics. Don't request permissions in batches at launch.

---

#### 1.12 Progressive Profiling

**Definition:** Don't ask everything upfront. Learn from behavior over time.

**Why this principle exists:** Onboarding completion rates die after 5 questions. Users who answer fewer questions during onboarding stay longer and engage more.

**Application:**
- Onboarding asks 4 things: goal, experience level, frequency, health data access. Skip is allowed on 3 of 4.
- Body weight, body fat, training history are NOT collected upfront -- they appear naturally when first needed
- Diet preferences, allergies, supplements: deferred to Settings, user adds when ready
- AI recommendations improve as the user logs more -- the system learns rather than asking

**Do:** Collect minimum viable data upfront. Learn the rest from behavior.

**Don't:** Force users through a 10-question profiling form before they can use the app.

---

#### 1.13 Celebration Not Guilt

**Definition:** Highlight streaks, PRs, and effort. Never shame missed days or unmet goals.

**Why this principle exists:** Fitness apps that punish users for missing days lose users. Apps that celebrate consistency keep users. Strava's kudos system, Apple's activity rings, and Duolingo's streak savers all use positive reinforcement.

**Application:**
- Missed workout: silent. No "you skipped Tuesday" notification.
- Streak indicator shows consecutive days, never "longest broken streak"
- PR detection: animation + haptic + temporary celebration card on home
- Rest days are explicitly "Recovery Day" -- not "Day Off" or "No Workout"
- Macro under-target: shows remaining, not "you missed your goal"

**Do:** Celebrate effort. Treat rest as part of training.

**Don't:** Use red text for "missed." Don't send guilt-trip notifications. Don't compare users to other users.

---

### How to Apply These Principles

When designing any new feature, the UX research phase walks through this checklist:

1. **Which principles apply?** Not all 13 apply to every feature. Identify the relevant ones.
2. **How does each apply?** Concrete design decision per principle.
3. **Where could violations happen?** Pre-emptively flag risks.
4. **What's the test?** How will you verify the principle is honored in the final implementation?

This produces a "Principle Application Table" in every UX spec -- a grid of principles, decisions, and verification criteria that travels with the feature from design through implementation.

## Part 2: Information Architecture

### The Fundamental Axis: Today vs. History

The app organizes around **temporal context** -- the same axis used by every successful fitness app:

- **Today** (the active state): What should I do? How am I doing? What's my readiness?
- **History** (the archive): How did I do? What patterns are emerging? What progressed?

Every screen falls on one side. The home screen, training plan, and nutrition tracker are **today** surfaces. The stats hub, workout history, and progress charts are **history** surfaces.

### Tab Bar Structure

A 4-tab bottom bar provides the primary navigation:

| Tab | Purpose | Side of Axis |
|-----|---------|--------------|
| **Home** | Readiness, today's plan, quick actions, 7-day trends | Today |
| **Training** | Today's session, exercise logging, program view | Today |
| **Nutrition** | Today's macros, meal log, supplement tracking | Today |
| **Stats** | Charts, metrics, trends, history | History |

Settings and account are accessed via an account avatar button in the toolbar -- not a 5th tab. This keeps the tab bar focused on daily-use surfaces.

**Why 4 tabs, not 5?**
- Hick's Law: 4 options is the cognitive sweet spot
- Thumb reach: 4 tabs at standard screen width keeps each tab in comfortable range
- Platform guidelines recommend 3-5 tabs

### Depth Limit Rule

**Maximum navigation depth: 3 levels from any tab.**

- Tab to Section to Detail (3 levels -- OK)
- Tab to Section to Detail to Sub-detail (4 levels -- NOT OK)

Beyond 3 levels, users lose context. If a feature needs more depth, it belongs in a modal or sheet, not pushed deeper.

### Modal vs. Push Navigation

**Push navigation** for reading flows -- drilling into existing content (stats to chart, exercise to detail).

**Sheets / modals** for creation and editing flows -- creating new content (add meal, add biometric, edit profile).

**Full-screen modals** reserved for immersive flows (active workout, onboarding, destructive confirmations).

### Cross-Domain Connections

Some content lives in multiple places. The rule: **edit in canonical, view from references.** Never duplicate edit surfaces.

| Content | Canonical Location | Referenced From |
|---------|-------------------|------------------|
| Today's readiness | Home | Training (adjusts session), Stats (recovery section) |
| Macros today | Nutrition | Home (quick action), Stats (nutrition section) |
| Training program | Training | Home (today's plan), Settings (program selection) |
| Health platform data | Health integration | Home, Stats, Training |

## Part 3: Interaction Patterns

### Navigation Patterns

| Pattern | When to Use | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Tab switch | Top-level destination change | Home to Training |
| Push | Drilling into existing content | Stats to Chart Detail |
| Sheet | Creating/editing content | Add meal |
| Full-screen modal | Immersive flow requiring focus | Active workout, onboarding |
| Confirmation modal | Destructive action verification | Delete account |

### Input Patterns

**Numeric Entry:**
- Small range (1-30): Stepper (e.g., reps per set)
- Large range with precision: Text field with numeric pad (e.g., weight)
- Bounded continuous: Slider (e.g., body fat % during onboarding)
- Discrete options: Picker or segmented control

**Auto-population is mandatory:** Set logging always pre-fills weight and reps from the most recent session. Users adjust if needed.

**Selection:**
- 2 options: Toggle or 2 buttons
- 2-3 options: Segmented control
- 4-6 options: Cards in a grid
- 7+ options: Searchable list (Hick's Law: never a flat picker without search)

### Feedback Patterns

**Haptic Feedback Taxonomy:**

| Event | Intensity | When |
|-------|-----------|------|
| Button press | Light | Every button tap |
| Selection change | Light | Picker/segmented changes |
| Set completed | Standard | Completing a workout set |
| Workout finished | Standard (double tap) | End of session |
| Personal record | Strong (custom sequence) | New PR achieved |
| Validation error | Standard | Input error |
| Destructive confirmation | Standard | Before destructive action |

**Rules:**
- Haptics are opt-out, not opt-in (enabled by default)
- Sound is always off by default (gym etiquette)
- All haptics respect the system Reduce Motion setting

**Toast Notifications (Non-Blocking):**
- Position: bottom-center, above tab bar
- Duration: 2.0s default, 3.5s for actionable toasts (with undo)
- Single line, max 40 characters
- Used for: "Workout saved", "Meal added", "Synced"

### Gesture Patterns

| Gesture | Action | Alternative |
|---------|--------|-------------|
| Tap | Primary action | -- |
| Long press | Context menu | Menu button |
| Swipe left | Destructive action | Long-press menu |
| Swipe right | Positive action | Tap |
| Swipe down (sheet) | Dismiss | Cancel button |
| Pull down (list) | Refresh | Manual sync button |
| Pinch | Zoom chart range | Period selector |
| Drag | Reorder list items | Long-press menu |

**Rule (Motor Accessibility):** Every gesture must have a non-gesture alternative. Users with motor impairments cannot reliably perform swipes or long-presses.

## Part 4: Data Visualization

### Chart Type Selection

| Data Type | Chart Type | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| Time-series trend | Line chart | Body weight over 30 days |
| Discrete comparison | Bar chart | Weekly volume per muscle group |
| Progress to goal | Ring/donut | Today's protein target (75/150g) |
| Compact trend | Sparkline | 7-day HRV trend next to current value |
| Consistency over time | Heat calendar | Workout frequency (GitHub-style) |

### Color Semantics in Data

| Meaning | When |
|---------|------|
| Brand accent | Active, in-progress |
| Success green | On target, positive trend, completed |
| Warning amber | Approaching limit, attention needed |
| Error red | Over limit, missed, error |
| Info blue | Informational, neutral, recovery |

**Trend direction is contextual.** The same value going up can be good or bad depending on the metric and the user's goal. Weight going up during a bulk: green. Weight going up during a cut: red. The system knows the goal and colors accordingly.

### Chart Accessibility

Every chart MUST have a text alternative:
- Screen reader users hear a summary: "Bench press 1RM, increased 5% over the last 4 weeks, current value 100 kilograms"
- Full data table available via long-press or screen reader custom action
- Color is never the only indicator -- always paired with shape, label, or position

## Part 5: Permission and Trust

### Core Principle

> Request permission at the moment of relevance, not during onboarding. Explain why before showing the system dialog.

System permission dialogs are one-shot. If a user denies, you cannot ask again. This makes the first request critical.

### The 3-Step Priming Pattern

Every permission follows this flow:

1. **Pre-Primer Screen** -- app-branded explanation of the benefit ("get reminded when rest is over"), not the mechanism ("we need notification access"). Two clear options: "Allow" and "Not Now."
2. **System Dialog** -- the native permission prompt.
3. **Graceful Degradation** -- if denied, the feature works in degraded mode with a non-intrusive re-enable banner.

**Critical: NEVER request multiple permissions at once.** Each permission has its own contextual moment. Batching destroys trust.

### Trust Signals

Visible markers that reinforce the privacy commitment:

| Signal | Where |
|--------|-------|
| Encryption badge | Security settings |
| "Stays on device" label | Health data permission screen |
| Privacy summary | Consent screen |
| Sync status indicator | App toolbar |
| Privacy policy link | Every data screen footer |

**Rule:** Privacy is a feature, not a disclosure. Surface it as a benefit, not a legal requirement.

## Part 6: State Patterns

Every screen has 5 possible states. The default state is the easy one. The other 4 are where most apps fail.

### The 5 States

| State | When |
|-------|------|
| **Default** | Normal content present |
| **Loading** | Async operation in progress |
| **Empty** | No content yet (first use, no data) |
| **Error** | Operation failed |
| **Success** | Operation succeeded (transient) |

### Loading States

| Duration | Treatment |
|----------|-----------|
| Under 200ms | No loading state (instant feels better) |
| 200-500ms | Skeleton or fade |
| 500ms+ | Explicit loader with progress or message |
| Over 5 seconds | Progress percentage if possible, cancel option if appropriate |

### Empty States

Empty states are the most-seen screen for new users. Treat them as primary onboarding surfaces, not error pages.

**Copy formula:**
1. What is missing (factual, no judgment)
2. What to do next (specific action)
3. Optional: why it matters (benefit, not pressure)

**Don't** say "You haven't logged anything yet" (judgmental). **Do** say "Your workout history will appear here. Log your first session to start tracking."

### Error States

Error states are a trust test. They reveal whether the app is forgiving or punishing.

**Copy formula:**
1. State the issue clearly, no jargon
2. State the consequence ("Your data is safe locally")
3. State the next action (what to tap)

**Never** show raw error messages. **Never** blame the user. **Always** reassure that data is safe and offer a clear recovery path.

## Part 7: Accessibility

Accessibility is not optional. It is a baseline requirement.

### Visual Accessibility

- Minimum 4.5:1 contrast for body text (WCAG AA)
- Minimum 3:1 for large text and UI components
- AAA (7:1) preferred for primary text
- All text uses Dynamic Type tokens -- scales automatically with system settings
- Tested at the largest accessibility text size before shipping

### Motor Accessibility

- All interactive elements: minimum 44x44pt tap targets
- Minimum 8pt spacing between adjacent targets
- No time-limited interactions (except user-controlled rest timer)
- Every gesture has a non-gesture alternative
- Gym-specific considerations: larger primary CTAs (52pt), bottom-anchored actions, full-row tap targets for set logging, auto-population to minimize typing

### Cognitive Accessibility

- Maximum 5-7 actionable items per view
- Consistent placement: primary CTA always bottom, back button always top-left, save/done always top-right of modals
- One primary action per screen (visually dominant)
- Auto-populate everything possible to remove recall burden
- All destructive actions are reversible (grace periods, undo toasts, explicit choice dialogs)

### Screen Reader Strategy

- Every interactive element has an accessibility label
- Every non-trivial action has an accessibility hint
- Charts have text summaries
- Custom components have custom accessibility actions
- Decorative images are hidden from the accessibility tree

## Part 8: Micro-Interactions and Motion

### Animation Principles

Every animation must communicate something. If it doesn't fall into one of these categories, remove it:

| Purpose | When |
|---------|------|
| State change | User changed something (toggle, checkbox) |
| Spatial transition | User moved between contexts (push, sheet) |
| Feedback | User performed an action (button press, save) |
| Attention | System wants user attention (badge, notification) |
| Celebration | User accomplished something significant (PR, streak) |

### Duration Guidelines

| Speed | Duration | Use |
|-------|----------|-----|
| Instant | 0.1s | Button press, opacity fade |
| Micro | 0.15s | Toggle flip, small state change |
| Short | 0.2s | Card interaction, segment switch |
| Standard | 0.3s | Page transition, sheet appear |
| Long | 0.5s | Emphasis, glow effect |
| Extended | 0.6s | Celebration animation |

Anything over 0.6s feels slow. Anything under 0.1s is invisible.

### Reduce Motion

When the system Reduce Motion setting is enabled:
- Slides become fades
- Scale animations are dropped
- Celebration animations skip or show a static badge
- Loading animations dim to a static state

This is non-negotiable. Motion that can't be reduced is motion that excludes users.

### Sound

**Sound is OFF by default.** Always. Users open the app in the gym. The only exception is an opt-in rest timer alarm that respects the system silent mode.

## Part 9: Content Strategy

### Tone and Voice

**Voice attributes:** Calm, direct, secure, supportive, non-hype.

| Avoid | Use Instead |
|-------|-------------|
| "Crush it" / "Beast mode" / "No excuses" | "Great work today" |
| "You failed to log a workout" | "Recovery day -- pick up tomorrow" |
| "Don't break the chain!" | (silent -- never guilt-trip) |
| "You're 200 calories over your limit" | "200 calories above today's target" |
| "Burn fat faster" | "Track energy balance over time" |

### Number Formatting

| Data | Format | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Weight (kg) | 1 decimal | 75.5 kg |
| Reps | Integer | 12 reps |
| Calories | Integer with separator | 2,150 cal |
| Macros | Integer | 150g protein |
| Body fat | 1 decimal | 18.5% |
| Duration (workout) | Hours + minutes | 1h 15m |
| Duration (rest) | mm:ss | 01:30 |
| HRV | Integer | 48 ms |
| Recent dates | Relative | Today, Yesterday, 3 days ago |

### Health Data Sensitivity

Health data carries emotional weight. A "weight" number isn't just data -- it can trigger anxiety, body image issues, or motivation collapse.

**Rules:**
1. Never compare users to other users
2. Never frame missing data as failure
3. Show context, not just numbers ("75 kg, down 0.5 kg this week")
4. Respect rest as part of training ("Recovery Day", not "Day Off")
5. Avoid triggering language around body image
6. Allow users to hide sensitive metrics from the home screen
7. Default to private -- no public profiles or social features

## Part 10: Platform Considerations

### Primary Platform (iPhone)

- Primary CTAs anchor to the bottom third (thumb zone)
- Tab bar at bottom (platform standard)
- All screens respect safe areas (status bar, home indicator, Dynamic Island)
- Tested across compact, baseline, and large device widths

### Secondary Platform (iPad)

- Adaptive layout switches from bottom tabs to sidebar navigation
- Split view for list/detail flows
- More information density where screen space allows
- Known gaps documented for iterative improvement

### Future Platforms

- Watch app planned for post-launch (readiness complication, rest timer, quick logging)
- Desktop builds via Mac Catalyst (functional, not optimized)
- Cross-platform token mapping complete for potential Android expansion

## Key Takeaways

- **13 principles, not arbitrary.** 8 universal heuristics (Fitts's Law through Error Prevention) plus 5 domain-specific principles (Readiness-First through Celebration Not Guilt) create a complete decision framework for fitness UX.
- **"Celebration Not Guilt" is the sleeper principle.** Most fitness apps default to shame mechanics (broken streaks, missed days, "you're behind"). Inverting this drives retention.
- **Every screen has 5 states, not 1.** Default, Loading, Empty, Error, and Success. Most apps only design the default. The other 4 are where users form trust opinions.
- **Privacy is a feature, not a disclosure.** Surfacing encryption badges and "stays on device" labels converts a legal obligation into a competitive advantage.
- **The Principle Application Table travels with the feature.** Every UX spec includes a grid of which principles apply and how -- making compliance auditable, not aspirational.
